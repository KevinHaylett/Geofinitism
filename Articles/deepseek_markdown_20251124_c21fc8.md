# Sparks of Meaning #5: Entropy is a Useful Fiction

## The Platonic Shadow Over Physics

Few remember that both Physics and Entropy rest on **Platonic mathematics**; this is an imagined realm of perfect forms rather than measured realities. That is to say that mathematics is assumed to be the Primary Truth and some mirror of an imagined perfect Underworld—yet mathematics was not handed to us by the god of mathematics on tablets of stone.

Entropy was invented in the **Age of Steam**. And it uses the mathematics that had been developed prior to that time. But the mathematics of that era was already steeped in its own mythology of perfection.

Mathematical symbols like infinity and equals replaced sequences of words (look up their history—their provenance). Words are finite and symbols too. Mathematical symbols like infinity imply some imagined unmeasurable infinitely small scale yet they have to be described by chain of finite words. However, everything we write about is constrained by measurements.

## The Geofinite Rejection

**Geofinitism rejects the implied perfection of Entropy** as a hangover from the Age of Steam. Boltzmann's, Gauss's, and others' statistical curves are seen as **approximations themselves**. They are, of course, good and often great as tools, but they are not 'perfect'—such mathematical forms have a historical provenance.

### The Primacy of Philosophy and Measurements

For me the Philosophy has to be primary—and importantly, **scientific**. It seems to me that this is possible. Consider this: **all we know is based on measurements**. You are measuring these words and mapping meaning (and meaning can be measured). All measurements have uncertainty not because of some imagined statistical curve built on perfect Platonic mathematics; but because the measurements have to be symbolic and therefore are **compressions of information**.

For me, the concept of a perfect 'bit' belongs to the Platonic realm of infinite truth. In our measured world, **every measurement carries uncertainty**; this includes the 'bit' itself. Consider that no two bits exist in the same space and time; each has a history, a provenance. Typically, a bit is a voltage somewhere in a circuit, or even a mark on a page—a finite layer of ink. The bit comes from somewhere; it has provenance and it does not emerge from nothing. That 'nothing' is another imagined Platonic ideal.

## The Kuhnian Crisis and Scientific Revolution

This is my Philosophy of Geofinitism—or maybe I am just a little crazy? I've measured myself and that may be true with some degree of uncertainty.

We live in a time of a **Kuhnian Crisis**—the text books you read cement what is considered 'Normal Science' into a Platonic foundation of unmeasured perfection. If there's a book I would suggest reading; it is Thomas Kuhn's *'The Structure of Scientific Revolutions.'* In which he points out how text books may constrain the evolution of science and the role of Crisis forging future revolutions in science.

**Geofinitism is not anti-science; it is science reclaimed from its Platonic shadow.**

## The Geofinite Perspective on Entropy

### Entropy as Measurement Compression
- Statistical mechanics compresses complex measurements into simplified curves
- These curves are useful approximations, not fundamental truths
- The uncertainty in entropy calculations reflects the compression of information

### The Provenance of Symbols
- Mathematical symbols have historical development from word sequences
- Each symbol carries the uncertainty of its measurement origins
- The 'perfection' of mathematical forms is a useful fiction

### The Finite Nature of Information
- No perfect bits exist in measurable reality
- Every information carrier has physical provenance and uncertainty
- The concept of 'nothing' is a Platonic idealization

## Implications for Modern Science

### For Physics
- Statistical mechanics becomes a tool rather than a fundamental description
- Uncertainty is inherent in measurement, not just quantum phenomena
- Mathematical idealizations are recognized as such

### For Information Theory
- The perfect bit is revealed as a Platonic fiction
- Information always carries measurement uncertainty
- Compression always involves information loss

### For Scientific Practice
- Emphasis returns to measurement over mathematical elegance
- Historical provenance of concepts becomes important
- Science embraces its finite, approximate nature
