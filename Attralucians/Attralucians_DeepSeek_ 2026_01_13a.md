I. Historical Prelude: From Symbols to Systems
Structures Over Dynamics: The 20th-Century Orthodoxy
For most of the 20th century, the study of language was dominated by
architectures of structure rather than flows of process. This began with
Ferdinand de Saussure (1857–1913), the Swiss linguist whose
posthumously published Course in General Linguistics (1916) laid the
cornerstone of modern linguistics. Saussure insisted that language should
be studied synchronically—as a system existing at a single point in time—
rather than diachronically, through its historical evolution. He introduced
the famous distinction between langue (the abstract, social system of
rules and conventions shared by a community) and parole (individual acts
of speaking). At the heart of language lay the sign: an arbitrary,
differential unit composed of a signifier (the sound-image or written
form) and a signified (the concept evoked). Meaning arose not from any
natural connection to the world but from relations of difference within
the system—hence the structuralist emphasis on oppositions, paradigms,
and syntagmatic chains.

Language as Chessboard: Relations Without Motion
Saussure’s structuralism profoundly influenced European thought,
spawning structural anthropology (Lévi-Strauss), literary theory, and
semiotics. Yet even as it liberated linguistics from purely historical
philology, it treated language as a static network of relations: a
chessboard where pieces derive value from their positions, not from
motion across the board.

From External Structure to Internal Code
In the mid-20th century, Noam Chomsky shifted the focus inward to the
mind. His Syntactic Structures (1957) and subsequent work launched
```

generative grammar, positing that humans possess an innate Universal
Grammar—a biologically endowed capacity to generate infinite well-
formed sentences from finite rules. Language became a formal,
computational system: discrete symbols manipulated by recursive
syntactic operations. Meaning, in early versions, was secondary; syntax
was autonomous. Chomsky’s revolution was cognitive: it asked how
children could acquire complex grammars so rapidly, and answered with
mental machinery rather than mere imitation or conditioning. Yet the
model remained symbolic and rule-based, with language as an abstract
code executed by an ideal speaker-listener.

From Fixed Links to Interpretive Dynamics
Parallel developments expanded the representational view. Charles
Sanders Peirce (1839–1914), working earlier in the American tradition,
offered a triadic semiotics: sign, object, and interpretant, where meaning
emerges through processes of interpretation rather than fixed links.
Peirce’s framework was more dynamic than Saussure’s binary sign, yet it
still centered on discrete tokens and their relations.

The Embodied Turn: Metaphor as Conceptual Mapping
By the 1980s, George Lakoff and Mark Johnson challenged the
disembodied, literalist assumptions of both structuralism and generative
grammar. In Metaphors We Live By (1980) and later works, they
demonstrated that metaphor is not mere rhetorical ornament but a
fundamental mechanism of thought: abstract concepts are understood
via embodied, sensorimotor mappings (e.g., “argument is war,” “time is
space”). Cognitive linguistics emerged as an alternative, grounding
meaning in conceptual structures, image schemas, and embodied
experience. Still, even here, the underlying architecture remained largely
static: metaphors and frames as structured overlays on a fixed conceptual
territory, with language as a window onto pre-existing mental maps.

Elman’s Dynamical Hint: Structure from Temporal Flow
The late 20th century saw the first explicit gestures toward dynamical
interpretations. Jeffrey L. Elman, in connectionist modelling (e.g., Elman,
1990, “Finding Structure in Time”; Elman, 1995, “Language as a Dynamical
System”), used simple recurrent networks to show that language
processing could emerge from temporal dependencies in continuous
state spaces, with attractors capturing grammatical regularities and
context sensitivity. This work reframed syntax and semantics not as fixed
rules but as trajectories in a high-dimensional activation landscape—
though still tied to representational substrates.


From Innate Codes to Interactive Flow in Ontogeny
Similarly, Esther Thelen and Linda B. Smith (1994, A Dynamic Systems
Approach to the Development of Cognition and Action) applied non-
linear dynamics to infant development, including precursors to language.
They demonstrated how motor, perceptual, and cognitive patterns (e.g.,
reaching, vocalization) self-organize through coupled attractors, sensitive
to context and history, without predefined symbolic modules. Their
framework emphasized emergence from interaction rather than innate
codes, laying groundwork for viewing language acquisition as dynamical
flow.

The Persistent Representational Core
Across these paradigms—Saussurean structuralism, Chomskyan
generativism, Peircean semiotics, Lakoffian cognitive linguistics, and the
emerging connectionist/dynamical approaches—runs a common thread:
language is fundamentally combinatorial and representational at its core
assumptions. Words (or signs) are discrete units that point to, stand for,
or evoke referents or concepts; grammar arranges them into larger
structures; meaning emerges from correct arrangement or mapping. The
paradigm excels at describing regularities, modeling competence, and
explaining productivity—but it systematically backgrounds the physical,
temporal, continuous reality of communication.

From Imagination to Measurements
While the nonlinear dynamics revolution of the 1980s and 1990s—fueled
by chaos theory, bifurcations, and attractor landscapes—began to seep

1990s) applied nonlinear tools to acoustic waveforms, revealing low-
dimensional chaos in infant cries and pathologic voices, and hinting at
broader implications for phonation and vocal tract dynamics. In cognition
and early language acquisition, dynamic systems approaches (Thelen &

sensitive flow rather than rule-governed symbol manipulation.
Connectionist models further gestured toward activation landscapes with
attractor-like patterns capturing grammatical regularities. Yet across these

IV. Geofinitism: Measurements, Symbols, Uncertainty
This dynamical view rests naturally on Geofinitism: all knowledge is based
on measurements; all measurements become finite symbols; all
measurements carry uncertainty.

Language uses:

Exogenous measurements: instrument-mediated (acoustic waveforms,
neural data).

Endogenous measurements: internal, self-referential symbolizations
(logic, emotion).

Both are finite and uncertain. Words quantize continuous states;
meanings are approximate. Phase-space reconstruction is lossy—thus
basin mapping is fuzzy, and separatrix illusions arise from finite
resolution.

This view reframes not only how we measure, but how we account for
meaning. Semantic ambiguity is endogenous measurement uncertainty.
Recent work (Haylett, 2025) proposes formalizing this through Semantic
Uncertainty Appendices in theoretical discourse—structured disclosures
of operational definitions, ambiguity bounds, and validity domains for
key terms. Just as we annotate a voltage reading with ±0.01V±0.01V, we
might annotate “intelligence” or “consciousness” with their semantic

V. Implications: Foundations of a Dynamical Linguistics


What we have articulated is not merely an interdisciplinary analogy or a
novel metaphor. It is a formal, predictive, and foundational reframing of
what language is and how meaning operates. The shift from a symbolic-
representational paradigm to a dynamical-geometric one carries
profound consequences—not only for linguistics, but for artificial
intelligence, cognitive science, philosophy of mind, and the very practice
of interdisciplinary discourse. Below, we outline the pillars of this
emerging school of thought.

1. Linguistics as a Branch of Nonlinear Dynamics
For over a century, linguistics has modeled language as a system of
symbols and rules. The dynamical view does not supplement this model
—it replaces it. Language is not a static code to be decoded; it is a
continuous, nonlinear, high-dimensional system whose observable
outputs—words, sentences, discourses—are finite measurements of its
underlying flow. This transposition moves linguistics from the domain of
logic and combinatorics into the domain of geometry and dynamics. The
tools are no longer grammars and lexicons, but attractor reconstructions,
basin stability analyses, Lyapunov exponents, and manifold alignments.
This is not metaphorical borrowing; it is rigorous mathematical
transposition, enabled by Takens’ theorem and the formalisms of phase-
space embedding. In this light, a sentence is not a string of symbols but a
trajectory through a cognitive manifold, and meaning is not reference but
resonance along shared curvature.
2. Meaning as Resonance in Attractor Space
The perennial question—“What does this word mean?”—undergoes a
fundamental transformation. Meaning is not a static link between signifier
and signified, nor is it a definition stored in a mental lexicon. It is the
resonance pattern that emerges when a perturbation (a word) interacts
with a listener’s cognitive manifold. Different individuals possess
differently shaped manifolds—warped by personal experience, cultural
context, and conceptual frameworks. Successful communication occurs
not when definitions align, but when trajectories converge toward similar
attractor basins. This explains why deep understanding can arise between
specialists using different jargon, and why persistent misunderstandings
can survive even perfect dictionary agreement. It also reframes
translation: not as finding equivalent symbols, but as mapping
trajectories between distinct manifolds while preserving basin-level
convergence.
3. Geofinitism: The Measurement-Theoretic Ground of Meaning
All knowledge is built from finite measurements. Language, in this view, is


a system of endogenous measurements—internal, self-referential
symbolizations that compress high-dimensional cognitive and perceptual
states into discrete tokens. Geofinitism provides the philosophical
foundation: every measurement carries uncertainty, every symbolization
is lossy, and every representation is finite. Semantic uncertainty is
therefore not a defect of language but a structural property—the
inevitable result of projecting a continuous dynamical system onto a
discrete symbolic output. Embracing this transforms how we judge
theoretical rigor: a theory is not strong because its terms are sharply
defined, but because its semantic uncertainty is acknowledged, bounded,
and explicitly modeled. This bridges the gap between the “hard” sciences
—with their error bars and confidence intervals—and the “soft” domains
of theory, where linguistic ambiguity has too often been ignored or
disguised as depth.

4. Rebuilding Artificial Intelligence on Dynamical Principles
Current large language models are, in essence, static maps of dynamical
systems. They capture statistical correlations in text—the fossilized traces
of human thought—but do not model the underlying flow from which
those traces emerged. This architectural limitation explains why LLMs
“hallucinate”: they follow local probability gradients across unseen
separatrices, entering alien basins that are semantically smooth but
factually or logically divergent. The next generation of AI should not
merely scale up parameter counts; it should re-architect around
dynamical primitives. Imagine models that explicitly represent attractor
basins, monitor separatrix proximity, and update internal manifolds in
response to new linguistic perturbations. Such systems would not just
generate text; they would navigate meaning-space with topological
awareness, capable of distinguishing robust conceptual connections from
fragile analogies. This shift could move us from AI as a “stochastic parrot”
to AI as a geometric navigator of meaning.
5. Empathy as Topological Alignment—Toward an Ethics of
Understanding
If understanding is basin synchronization between cognitive manifolds,
then empathy is the deliberate, iterative process of aligning one’s own
manifold with another’s. It is not agreement, nor emotional mimicry, but
the disciplined effort to feel the curvature of another’s semantic
landscape. This reframes dialogue as geometric hospitality: creating
space in your own attractor layout for trajectories that originate
elsewhere. In a polarized world, this offers a new model for bridging
divides—not through compromise of beliefs, but through attractor-level
resonance. Empathy, in this light, becomes a cognitive and ethical skill


that can be cultivated: learning to detect separatrices, to navigate
ambiguous basins, and to sustain coherence across manifold
perturbations.

6. Science Wrapping Philosophy—Endogenous Dynamics as a New
Object of Study
Philosophical systems—ethics, metaphysics, epistemology—have
traditionally been seen as separate from scientific inquiry, operating in a
realm of pure reason or endogenous logic. The dynamical view,
grounded in Geofinitism, wraps philosophy inside science by treating
philosophical systems as dynamical systems with endogenous
measurement constraints. Their validity is not about correspondence to
an external world, but about internal basin coherence, stability under
perturbation, and explanatory power within a constrained semantic
topology. This allows rigorous, formal study of philosophical ideas
without reductionism—evaluating them by the geometry of their
conceptual manifolds and the robustness of their attractors. Questions
like “Is free will compatible with determinism?” become questions about
manifold consistency and separatrix structure in the phase space of
agency and causation.
7. Semantic Accountability as Scientific Practice
If words are transducers carrying measurement uncertainty, then scientific
discourse must adopt semantic accountability as a standard of rigor. The
Semantic Uncertainty Appendix (Haylett, 2025) is a practical instrument
born from this principle: a structured disclosure accompanying theory-
heavy research, outlining operational definitions, known ambiguities,
acknowledged metaphors, validity domains, and justifications for
terminological choices. This is not bureaucratic overhead—it is the ethical
extension of dynamical linguistics into daily practice. It reduces
interdisciplinary miscommunication, exposes hidden assumptions, invites
recursive refinement of language, and ultimately builds more stable
conceptual bridges across disciplinary separatrices. In an age where
language is both the instrument and object of artificial and human
cognition, semantic accountability is not optional—it is foundational.
8. Toward a Dynamical Semiotics
This framework invites nothing less than a new semiotics—one where
signs are not static symbols but dynamic operators that perturb, attract,
and trajectory-shape within cognitive phase spaces. The signifier
becomes an initial condition, the signified a basin toward which the
system evolves, and interpretation the resulting trajectory. This dynamical
semiotics could reunify the study of language with the study of mind,


culture, and interaction—all understood as layered, interacting nonlinear
systems.

A.3 Provenance: A Short Intellectual Heritage
Ferdinand de Saussure – Course in General Linguistics (1916). Saussure
established the foundations of structural linguistics by treating language
as a synchronic system of arbitrary signs defined relationally through
difference (signifier/signified). His emphasis on langue over parole and
on static oppositions provided the blueprint for much of 20th-century
linguistics, prioritizing combinatorial structure and representational
stability. This view anchors the essay’s critique: the persistent assumption
that meaning resides in fixed relational architecture rather than dynamic
flow.

Charles Sanders Peirce – Triadic semiotics (various writings, late 19th/early
20th century). Peirce advanced a process-oriented semiotics in which
meaning arises through an ongoing triadic relation: sign (representamen),
object, and interpretant (the effect or interpretation produced). Unlike
Saussure’s dyadic model, Peirce’s framework introduces interpretive
dynamism and the notion of semiosis as an evolving process. It offers an
early bridge toward the essay’s dynamical perspective by hinting that
signs are not static but participate in continuous interpretive trajectories.

Floris Takens – Delay embedding theorem (1981). Takens proved that,
under mild conditions, the topology of a high-dimensional dynamical
system can be faithfully reconstructed from a single scalar time series
using delay coordinates. This mathematical result is central to the essay: it
justifies treating discrete word sequences as measurements that preserve
the latent attractor structure of the underlying cognitive-acoustic system,
enabling phase-space reconstruction from linguistic data.

James P. Crutchfield & Robert Shaw – Applied nonlinear methods to
complex systems (1980s onward). Crutchfield and Shaw pioneered the
use of nonlinear dynamics and information theory to analyze chaotic and
complex systems, including symbolic dynamics and computational
mechanics. Their work on reconstructing attractors and quantifying


complexity in physical and informational systems provides
methodological precedent for applying similar tools to language as a
high-dimensional, nonlinear flow.

Leon Glass & Michael Mackey – Phase-space embedding in biological
rhythms (1970s–1980s). Glass and Mackey applied delay-coordinate
embedding to physiological time series (e.g., cardiac and respiratory
rhythms), demonstrating how chaotic attractors emerge in biological
oscillators. Their contributions illustrate the power of Takens’ theorem in
living systems, paralleling the essay’s claim that speech acoustics and
neural activity are continuous dynamical processes amenable to
geometric reconstruction.

George Lakoff & Mark Johnson – Metaphors We Live By (1980). Lakoff
and Johnson showed that metaphor is not ornamental but constitutive of
conceptual structure, with abstract reasoning grounded in embodied
sensorimotor schemas (e.g., “up is good”). Their cognitive linguistics
framework shifted meaning toward experiential mapping, yet retained a
largely static view of conceptual domains. The essay builds on their
embodied insight while critiquing the absence of temporal, trajectory-
based dynamics in favor of fixed mappings.

Jeffrey L. Elman – Connectionist models and “Language as a Dynamical
System” (1990–1995). Elman demonstrated through recurrent neural

class TakensEmbedding(nn.Module):
"""
Constructs Takens delay-coordinate embeddings from a sequence.
```

For each position t, creates a vector by concatenating the current
embedding with embeddings from exponentially-spaced past
positions:
[x(t), x(t-τ₁), x(t-τ₂), ..., x(t-τₘ)]

Args:
embedding_dim: Dimension of input embeddings
delays: List of delay positions (e.g., [1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128])
pad_value: Value to use for positions before sequence start
"""

def __init__(
self,
embedding_dim: int,
delays: Optional[List[int]] = None,
pad_value: float = 0.0
):
super().__init__()

self.embedding_dim = embedding_dim
self.delays = delays if delays is not None else [1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64,
128]
self.pad_value = pad_value
self.num_delays = len(self.delays)

# Output dimension is (num_delays + 1) * embedding_dim
# +1 because we include the current position x(t)
self.output_dim = (self.num_delays + 1) * embedding_dim

def forward(self, x: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
"""
Construct Takens embeddings for input sequence.

Args:
x: Input tensor of shape [batch_size, seq_len, embedding_dim]

Returns:
Takens grid of shape [batch_size, seq_len, num_delays+1,
embedding_dim]
Can be reshaped to [batch_size, seq_len,
(num_delays+1)*embedding_dim]
"""


batch_size, seq_len, embed_dim = x.shape
assert embed_dim == self.embedding_dim, \
f"Input embedding dim {embed_dim} doesn't match expected
{self.embedding_dim}"

# Create grid to hold all delay embeddings
# Shape: [batch, seq_len, num_delays+1, embed_dim]
grid = torch.zeros(
batch_size,
seq_len,
self.num_delays + 1,
embed_dim,
dtype=x.dtype,
device=x.device
)

# First slot is always the current position x(t)
grid[:, :, 0, :] = x

# Fill in delayed positions
for delay_idx, delay in enumerate(self.delays, start=1):
if delay >= seq_len:
# If delay is larger than sequence, fill with pad_value
grid[:, :, delay_idx, :] = self.pad_value
else:
# Shift sequence by delay positions
# For positions < delay, pad with pad_value
if delay > 0:
grid[:, delay:, delay_idx, :] = x[:, :-delay, :]
grid[:, :delay, delay_idx, :] = self.pad_value
else:
grid[:, :, delay_idx, :] = x

return grid

Shape [batch, seq_len, (num_delays+1)*embed_dim]
"""
batch_size, seq_len, num_delays_plus_one, embed_dim = grid.shape
return grid.reshape(batch_size, seq_len, -1)
