A Discussion on the Measurement Problem

Author: Amihart

If you look at any list of unsolved problems in the physical
sciences, you will probably run across the measurement
problem. This problem is often misunderstood and
incorrectly characterized.

For example, quantum mechanics only gives you statistical
results in most cases and cannot make a deterministic
prediction, and so some people characterize the
measurement problem as “why do we get one outcome
rather than another?” This question is basically asking for a
hidden variable model which would, indeed,
deterministically explain why an experiment results in one
particular outcome rather than another.

Yet, orthodox quantum mechanics does not contain
hidden variables. Introducing them would require
modifying the mathematics of the theory. The answer to
the question in orthodox quantum mechanics is that
“there is no reason” because the outcome is treated as
irreducibly random.

The measurement problem concerns orthodox quantum
mechanics if we were to directly interpret it as a theory of
nature. It does not concern potential modifications to the
theory. That is to say, the measurement problem is only
applicable precisely in the case where we assume that
there are no hidden variables.


The measurement problem is also often falsely
characterized as the question “what causes the collapse of
the wavefunction?” However, this question is actually
jumping the gun. As we will discuss later, assuming that
the collapse of the wavefunction is a physical event

The Measurement Problem
I would argue that the measurement problem really comes
down to two contradictory statements. If the statements
contradict each other, then both cannot simultaneously be
correct. You thus must throw one of the two statements
out, and which one you throw out will determine the
character of the “solution” to the measurement problem.

The two statements are the following.

Making a measurement physically perturbs the system and
physically alters its state, as illustrated in the famous
double-slit experiment.
Any rigorous mathematical and local definition of a
measurement can be proved to necessarily contradict the
mathematical and thus empirical predictions of quantum
theory.
Popsci articles and videos often characterize quantum
mechanics as one where particles spread out as physical
waves which “collapse” into a single definite state when
you measure them, which implies the measurement itself
physically perturbs the system and alters its state, causing
it to collapse like a house of cards down to a simpler state.

However, I have written extensively in this article why this
point of view is actually not compatible with the
mathematics of quantum mechanics.

To put it simply, all physical interactions in quantum
mechanics are reversible, and so if we had a more complex
interaction described by a more complex system, like a
measuring device, this more complex interaction must
necessarily be reducible down to the “building blocks” of
reversible interactions, and thus must necessarily be
reversible.


Yet, the collapse of the wavefunction that occurs at
“measurement” is not a reversible interaction, so
introducing an interaction that is non-reversible must
inherently require introducing new mathematics to the
theory.

Hence, we end up with two seemingly contradictory
statements. How can “measurement” physically alter the
system, but giving any sort of physical description of what
constitutes a “measurement” is impossible without
contradicting the mathematics of the theory? The latter
implies that the measurement is not even a physical
process, but a non-physical process cannot meaningfully
perturb a system. Thus, a contradiction.

You have three choices to resolve the contradiction.

Drop the first premise and argue that all measurements in
quantum mechanics are passive and do not actually
perturb the system.
Drop locality from the second premise and accept that
things can influence each other faster than light.
Drop the second premise entirely and assume that
quantum mechanics is not a complete theory of nature
and it is okay to contradict with its mathematical and
empirical predictions.

Epistemic (Subjective) Collapse
Despite common misconception, the Copenhagen
interpretation does not argue that particles spread out as
waves until you look at them and then undergo a physical
collapse into particles. The original Copenhagen
interpretation was intended to be an epistemic
interpretation.

Let me explain what it means for the collapse of the
wavefunction to be epistemic or subjective. People often
falsely associate this with magical thinking, as if this is
some deep statement about “consciousness” controlling
the universe or something. We need to clear up
misconceptions about this.

Let’s say I flip a coin at time t=0. While it is in the air, Alice


will predict it has a 50%/50% distribution of landing on
heads/tails. Now, let’s say it lands at time t=1 and Alice
sees it lands on heads, Alice can then update her
prediction to 100%/0% for heads/tails since she can see
what it lands on.

Later, at time t=2, Alice asks Bob who did not see what it
landed on, and Bob will respond with a prediction of
50%/50% for heads/tails. Then, Alice tells Bob what she
saw at time t=3, and so Bob then can update his
prediction to 100%/0% for heads/tails.

At time t=2, Alice’s prediction is 100%/0% and Bob’s is
50%/50%. Does this mean that the coin is in a physically
different state for Alice and for Bob? No, because the
statistics does not describe the coin. It is an accounting of
their knowledge on the coin and thus a prediction based
on that knowledge of its state if they were to measure it.
Alice and Bob have different states of knowledge on the
coin.

When Bob acquires knowledge on the coin at the
transition from t=2 to t=3, his statistics also transition
from 50%/50% to 100%/0%. Does that prove the coin
physically changed its state just by Bob learning about it?
No, just reflects an update to Bob’s knowledge about the
coin. It reflects no physical change in the coin itself.

Indeed, in Copenhagen, the collapse of the wavefunction is
treated as an epistemic update of the observer’s
knowledge on the system. There is no claim that particles
physically spread out as waves. The wavefunction is just an
accounting of the observer’s knowledge about how the
system was prepared which can be used to predict what its
state might be if they were to measure it. When they
collapse the wavefunction after measurement, this is just
an update of what they know about the system.

The double-slit experiment is often used as proof that
measurements are not passive in quantum mechanics, but
there are also good arguments to believe that this may be
a poor conclusion. Let us now cover some arguments in
favor of an epistemic viewpoint.


Arguments #1: Schrodinger’s Cat and Wigner’s Friend
Consider our example with the coin in the previous section
at time t=1. If we take the wavefunction to be the physical
state of the system and thus its collapse to be a physical
event, then Alice and Bob’s different descriptions at this
time would reflect the system in a physically different state
from their different perspectives.

It is not inherently wrong to say that the state of the
system is different from different perspectives. Indeed, we
know that some properties differ between perspectives
such as velocity in Galilean relativity.

However, consider a Schrodinger’s cat type scenario,
where from the outside observer’s perspective, the “cat” in
the box is in a superposition of states. A superposition of
states does not mean that the cat is alive or dead, nor
does it mean the cat is both alive and dead.
Mathematically, it is a third thing, something that is
infinite-dimensional and described in terms of complex
numbers, and thus has no connection to our day-to-day
lives.

The “Wigner’s friend” thought experiment is a similar
thought experiment but where the cat in a box is replaced
with another person. This allows us to more clearly
introduce theory of mindto the picture. It is easier to
imagine placing ourselves into the perspective of another
person and asking what they may have been thinking or
feeling at that particular moment.

Yet, this question seems unanswerable if, from one
person’s perspective, the other is in a bizarre state that has
no connection to anything we are familiar with in the real
world. We cannot make any statements about what the
person might be seeing or feeling, as this inherently
requires us to assign definite states that are not a
superposition of states. We are unable to apply a theory of
mind to this scenario.

If the wavefunction is subjective, then there is no difficulty
in applying a theory of mind. The strange description of
the observer in the box from the one outside is just a way
of accounting for their knowledge of how the box was


initially prepared and does not reflect in any way a physical
statement about its contents, although it can be used to
make a statistical prediction as to its contents if they were
to go measure it.

Argument #2: Passive Measuring Devices Exist
Papers that describe the foundations of quantum
mechanics will often use quantum circuits to illustrate
certain “paradoxes.” The famous Frauchiger-Renner uses a
qubit to represent the internal state of a measurement
device and an operator called the CX operator (Controlled-
NOT) to represent the interaction with the measurement
device.

What is particularly interesting about this is the qubit + CX
interaction is clearly a passive one if we assume locality.

The matrix for the CX operator describes a deterministic
process where the target qubit is flipped if and only if the
control qubit is 1. The control qubit is thus not affected,
whereas the target qubit is. This works as a measurement
device because if the target qubit has an initial value of 0,
then it will be flipped to 1 if the control is 1, but remain 0 if
the control is 0, and thus the target is guaranteed to
record the value of the control.

This is clearly a passive process.

You might argue that it can be shown to be perturbing
because it affects the wavefunction, but the wavefunction
is a property of the whole system, and thus we would
inherently be introducing nonlocality. If we put the
wavefunction aside and just look at the actual definite
values of qubits or particles, it is a passive measurement.

Argument #3: Bell’s Theorem
With single-particle experiments, like the double-slit
experiment, it does indeed appear that the measurement
device is perturbing the system because the outcome of
the experiment depends upon how you measure the
system, called the measurement context.

It would be no surprise that the system’s behavior
depends upon the measurement context if the measuring


device perturbs the system. Of course, then, there would
be a dependence upon the behavior of the system and the
configuration of your measurement settings.

However, this argument becomes problematic if we
introduce more than one particle. Bell’s theorem proves it
is possible for a single particle’s behavior to be dependent
upon the configuration of multiple measurement devices
even if it only interacts with one of them.

We always associate perturbation with a local interaction
as that is the moment in time when one system perturbs
another. However, if we try to explain Bell’s theorem with
perturbation, we have to assume that one system can
perturb another without even interacting with it, which
then it becomes a bit confusing to explain when this even
actually occurs.

Whatever explanation you do give, you also run into
problems with special relativity.

Let’s say Alice and Bob have entangled particles and we
agree that the nonlocal perturbation occurs the moment
the first observer measures their particle and it nonlocally
perturbs the distant particle, assigning it a definite value at
that moment.

Since this is nonlocal, if the two measurements are space-
like separated events, then it is possible for there to be
different reference frames in special relativity that disagree
on who actually measured their particle first, and thus it
becomes ambiguous as which particle perturbed the other.

This “works” but feels rather strange and contrived.

Argument #4: Decoherence is Epistemic
Decoherence is sometimes presented as a way to describe
how a measurement device can produce an irreversible
interaction, but this is incorrect. It is mathematically
impossible in orthodox quantum mechanics for there to be
an irreversible interaction as all interactions are reversible.

Rather, decoherence is an epistemic phenomena whereby
you assume that the observer loses track of certain


relevant information needed to predict the behavior of the
system.

Decoherence works like this:

Consider the Schrodinger’s cat paradox where you have a
cat in the box and the observer on the outside. Let’s
assume that what the cat does is “measure” the Geiger
counter in the box. From the cat’s perspective, they will
observe a definite value on the Geiger counter. From the
person outside the box, they would describe the box in a
superposition of states for the possible outcomes of what
the cat observes, with the cat entangled with the Geiger
counter.

Now, all decoherence says is that if we take the entangled
superposition of states from the observer outside of the
box and assume that the cat lacks knowledge on itself, we
can “trace out” (remove) the cat from our description of
the box, and what we are left with is a classical probability
distribution of what the cat might be observing, no longer
described in superposition of states, i.e. you get an
irreversible change to the state of the system.

That is to say, all decoherence says the observer’s own
state plays a key role in predicting the behavior of a
system, but because they cannot keep track of their own
state, this gives the appearance of the system undergoing
an irreversible interaction. This is only an “appearance,”
though, to their ignorance of the full state of the system. If
the observer could keep track of their own state along
with that of the rest of the system, they would not
describe any irreversible interactions occurring.

The irreversible nature of decoherence is epistemic. If we
want to be more technical, all operators are unitary, and
irreversible interactions cannot be achieved through any
combination of unitary operators. Decoherence always
works by taking a density matrix that describes a system,
applying a unitary operator, and assuming that after you
applied it, the observer loses epistemic access to a part of
the system.

You then apply a partial trace to produce a reduced


density matrix which describes the subsystem with the part
the observer lost epistemic access to “traced out”
(removed). Only then does it appear that the unitary
operator produced an irreversible interaction, because you
are assuming that the observer could not keep track of
some information in the system.

Decoherence being epistemic thus does not imply the
measurement is active (perturbing) to what the observer is
trying to measure.
