**Ancient and Medieval Foundations**
The philosophy of language begins in antiquity with thinkers like Plato and Aristotle. Plato, in dialogues like
_Cratylus_ , debated whether words have a natural connection to their referents (naturalism) or are arbitrary
conventions (conventionalism). Aristotle, in _De Interpretatione_ , proposed that words are symbols of mental
experiences, which in turn reflect things in the world—a triadic relationship of word, thought, and reality. This
set the stage for viewing language as a structured system, a precursor to your geometric framing.
In the medieval period, thinkers like Thomas Aquinas and William of Ockham refined these ideas. Aquinas saw
language as a tool for expressing divine and human truths, while Ockham’s nominalism argued that universals

These early debates resonate with your notion of words as “magneto-words” in a semantic space, where
meaning emerges from relational interactions rather than inherent essences.

**The Linguistic Turn: 20th Century**
The 20th century marked the “linguistic turn” in philosophy, where language became central to understanding
thought and reality. Gottlob Frege revolutionized the field by distinguishing between a word’s sense (its
meaning within a linguistic system) and its reference (the object it denotes). His work laid the groundwork for
formal semantics, treating meaning as a structured relationship within a logical system—a step toward your
geometric conceptualization.
Ludwig Wittgenstein, perhaps the most influential figure in this era, offered two contrasting views. In his
_Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus_ (1921), he proposed a “picture theory” of language, where propositions mirror

a structured manifold. However, in his later _Philosophical Investigations_ (1953), Wittgenstein shifted to a “use

with your idea of language as a dynamic, interactional system.
Ferdinand de Saussure, a founder of structural linguistics, introduced the idea that language is a system of

Noam Chomsky’s generative grammar further formalized language as a rule-based system, with an innate
